Why do we hide and cover our rivers?

Plus: it snowed in the hills, the super bowl was in town, and there's a South Bay Journal event?!

Originally posted on substack on March 06, 2026

The world is defined by constant change and movement. I’m not talking about the world in any abstract sense, literally the ground below our feet. Wind, water, earth all shifting one another. It’s happening all the time. We’re ants living in a fragile ant maze, and the maze happens to be the living breathing organism of Earth.

What’s happened in the last two hundred years or so, though; is that humans have increasingly gained the ability to contend with the natural processes of the Earth. Large scale change in the world is no longer limited to earthquakes, floods, landslides, tsunamis, and all those other things. Now we have to include variations of these things caused by and created by humans . We build massive structures capable of shifting the crust of the Earth itself. Our intense extractive practices of mining and fracking have been shown to cause human-induced earthquakes in the midwest. We continue to engineer and encroach on floodplains and uncertain water bodies, increasing the likelihood of flooding in our cities. We change the earth in countless ways all the time. The ants are shifting the ant maze themselves, and the anthropocene is upon us more than ever.

Why all this has been on my mind lately is because of something I saw on the news recently. On January 20, the United Nations declared us (the proverbial ants) as being in a “global water bankruptcy” . This is what I imagine to be the precursor in some blockbuster movie about the water wars. The news anchor, shown in shock on a TV, announces global water bankruptcy, and the world is sent into a frenzy.

But it isn’t.

It’s hard to conceptualize global environmental problems, I don’t think anyone can think in scales that big. But in one sense, global systems are downstream of collective regional systems and how they work. And in turn, understanding the local regional scale is possibly more important comparatively, which is part of why I write this substack in the first place. So, this post is a practice in trying to learn just a little bit about the complex interconnected systems of water in the south bay. Where do we get water from, where does our water flow, how are we impacting the access to it, and what does it mean to all our daily lives, regionally or globally. Hopefully we can all pick up something new, instead of just having read drunken ramblings by me. And don’t worry, I’m just saving that for next week.

You could run a simulation of human history a million times, and I reckon us homo sapiens sapiens would inhabit this area in all but like two of those. This is the kind of geography you draw and dream up in an ideal fantasy world. A large natural harbor for building ports and connecting to the rest of the world, one of the largest on the western coast of North America. Lush and fertile valleys with enclosing mountains by the oceanside. Large coursing rivers from both the north and south. And the expansive yet shallow estuary that makes all the features before possible; the bay.

Our suburban shape lends itself to a bizarre realization as you go about your life in the south bay. Where are the rivers? Unless you’re at Alviso or some viewpoint on by the bay itself, it’s easy to ignore the bodies of water we live next to. You can go days without sighting the creeks or lakes that feed into the bay. Instead, we see concrete and office parks and expressways and parking lots and strip malls. The natural features are hard to spot, secondary to the urban make-up of the area. This data comes straight from the Santa Clara Valley Water District. Commonly referred to as SCWVD, they are a public entity that provides and handles water through groundwater extraction and import (not to be confused with private entities in the area such as CalWater who simply ‘sell’ the water). The sizes are exaggerated in the map above for visual purpose, but it’s a good look at the waterways and lakes that define the greater south bay. After snooping around online and observing the map for a while, here were some findings I had:

  1. The main rivers of the area are the Guadalupe River, Coyote Creek, Steven’s Creek, Pentencia Creek. Guadalupe River actually runs right by downtown San Jose, where the moldy and slimy waterfront by the Center for Performing Arts leaves a little to be desired.

  2. Water flows in from both the Santa Cruz mountains to the west and south, and the Diablo Range hills to the east. However, the creeks from the Santa Cruz Mountains are larger, less likely to be seasonal, and contribute more to the waters of the bay. This is because of higher precipitation in the Santa Cruz mountains. About a month ago, we also got to see snowfall in both these ranges during the late winter chills, up on Mt Hamilton and the Santa Cruz mountains. If you went by a creek in the days following the rains and the snow, they were flowing pretty decent.

  3. The density of minor creeks is understandably high in the mountains when compared to the cities. The urban creeks naturally funnel through channels but we also stop the ‘superfluous’ flows. In fact, a large part of this change is human-caused, which may be apparent with how incredibly straight some of them are… More on this a little later.

  4. We have a large amount of reservoirs that supply part of the drinking water in the area (the large San Luis Reservoir, the Calaveras Reservoir, the Stevens Creek Reservoir). A lot of our drinking water also comes from our groundwater supply, imported water, and aquifers all the way in Kern County. And, through the Santa Clara Valley, the Hetch-Hetchy Aqueduct , the source for San Francisco’s water, also runs through the area. If you look at an aerial map of the area, you can spot these odd long rectangular parcels of land that cut through with little development on them. That’s SFPUC property, carrying water from the flooded Yosemite valley reservoir of Hetch-Hetchey, all the way to the city.

  5. The salt ponds. The bright pink and red salt ponds were created and engineered largely in the 19th century, cover a little under 17,000 acres. There were native salt ponds made by the Ohlone people in the past, but these were not as large in their footprints. In the past few years, there has been an active effort to restore many of these to their marshy natural state again.

  6. Some of the water features we use recreationally are completely artificial, and not actually connected as a part of the water system. A few examples include Lake Cunningham, Shoreline Lake, and Almaden Lake. These lakes are still impacted by the greater water system though as water contamination can still occur through storm-drain systems and there’s always the threat of running dry during drought seasons (which is what happened to the beloved Memorial Park in Cupertino).

  7. Notice that one river which drains into Monterey Bay? That’s the Pajaro River, a historic outlet of the central valley. If you want to see something gnarly, look up the underwater Monterey Canyon which this river flows towards. An incredibly rare and deep gorge straight into depths of the ocean right by the coast, a large part of why the marine wildlife near the coast of Santa Cruz is so varied.

  8. If you’re generally interested in seeing historical maps to track the movement of some of these creeks, I found this website to be really great for this. Click a region on the map of the south bay that you’re interested in, and then a specific creek whose map you would like to see. One of the cooler things I found was an old slough named Devil’s Slough, right by Guadalupe River. That’s where one of the salt ponds is now.

  9. Many of the more prominent city, county, regional parks are situated right by the creeks on marshes or grasslands. This is why I question if continually covering them up and hiding their existence is such a great idea even in a purely selfish way, these are our primary recreational places in the area.

What’s interesting though is that many of the creeks no longer run their natural courses. Naturally, rivers shift and move as their water does. But in urban spaces, we dictate them. In Santa Clara County, many of our urban creeks and rivers can hardly be considered natural creeks for the majority of their stretches anymore. They get funneled into concrete pipes and culverts below large roads, tucked away from sight. They’re hidden in a very utilitarian way.

Have you ever driven on 237, heading west towards El Camino Real? Steven’s Creek is located below that monstrous interchange with highway 85, but it’s hard to notice even when taking the exit and looping around it. And at Main Street in Cupertino, Calabazas Creek flows underground straight through the development, and is still almost impossible to notice unless you’re looking out for it. You can only catch a glimpse of the culverts on Vallco and Steven’s Creek Boulevard if you look for it:

It all makes sense, it does. Way back when, rivers were just the sewers of cities across the country. We threw shit in, and it drained away somewhere and ceased to be an issue. Good enough. Slowly, we found out how diseases and pollution can travel through them, and we began to divert them a little smarter. Eventually, we sought complete control over these rivers for better or for worse.

I went over to the Mountain View library to investigate and pick something up from people who know about the local creeks, and to find photos. Turns out, until the mid 50s and 60s, many of the rivers and creeks here were relatively untouched. People have, of course, been living in the valleys surrounding the bay for millenia but it wasn’t until the Europeans arrived that nature began to be destabilized at a large and industrial scale.

We were still building overnight neighborhoods near them and roads to span across them but that was limited to relatively simple concrete bridges. We hadn’t altered them significantly, to the point of changing the flow yet.

Large flooding in 1955 and 1956 was what first spurred on the wrangling of creeks by SCVWD, the US Corps of Engineers, and the PW departments ofMountain View and Sunnyvale. The creeks that flooded were Permanente Creek and Steven’s Creek especially near the, at the time, brand new neighborhood of Rex-Manor. A particularly heavy rain season led to flooding along all their banks, and action was taken quickly. Entire homes were relocated, land was shifted, and a new path for the creek was devised. It’s not a long walk from the Great America VTA station to Ulistac. But by the time I reached, it had gotten hard to see anything very well. It was too dark. That’s the thing about winter, you can’t really do anything in the evening. Feels like you can’t do anything with your life on those days. But still, I walked to the gate with some purpose.

Ulistac Natural area is the only one of its kind in the city limits of Santa Clara. Walking or driving by, you’d be hard pressed to guess there was anything more than unkempt nature yet. And while it is unkempt, it is done so by design and practice - it is meant to be as untouched as a place in a major urban area can be. I feel like I’m in the hinterlands, even though I’m so close to everything.

A small path opens itself up, besides a sign labeling the storm pump.

I walk deeper into the park, no idea where I’m headed. There’s a ridge next to the river itself. It’s perfect for a view, and I walk beside it towards an overpass maybe a half mile away.

In the distance the lights of Levi’s Stadium flickered, making it clear to me that I haven’t left the city. In a few day’s time, the Super Bowl would be coming here. Roads were being shut down already, days and days in advance. People from across the country would fly in to witness the spectacle that is the Super Bowl. I wonder how many would make a visit to Ulistac.

As I continue walking, my wonder leaves me and instead my mind is filled with the relentless sound of frogs by the river. Preferable to car noise? Yes, just barely. Good thing I can tune it out; my ears are ringing all the time. The song of the frogs carries me to an opening where I can head back onto the overpass that flies over Guadalupe River.

I met this volunteer once, at a high school key club event. One of those volunteering organizations where you dicked around with people. We were at Coyote Creek, cleaning up the banks of the creek. Trash of all sorts. Plastic wrappers, dodgy needles, old magazines, random parts of tires ripped seemingly apart in aggressive fashion.

After getting closer to the creek, we had spotted a particularly old and dilapidated office chair in a thick group of shrubs. We sat down, waiting for more people to join us so that they could help us get it out. She sighed. We had waited for five minutes. She twiddled her thumbs. She sighed again.

“Maybe we should just keep it there. Everyone throws stuff in the creek. These people live here, I feel like maybe they’re allowed.”

Ever think about how people talk about the bay having “good access to nature”? While true from a certain perspective, it’s an interesting concept when you really stop and consider it. We clearly don’t think of nature as a place we live in, and we implicitly recognize that we live in spaces outside of it. Thus we go to nature. We visit nature. And we preserve the lands and lakes that enough of us deem as worthy enough to be nature . Like Steven’s Creek is a nice creek, and it’s surrounded by gorgeous hills for many stretches of it. Go hike by McLellan and Picchetti and it’s gorgeous after the rains. But not to the point of us preserving it along its path entirely. That wouldn’t make sense would it? We have a hierarchy for nature and it is directly related to the beauty or meaning we attribute to it, and more importantly, the value we gain from it. If the meaning or beauty can’t be quantified and charted, it is assumed to be zero. It’s become at the end of the day, we know photographers and tourists and poets won’t come from out the country to delight at the rush of the water and write about Babb Creek, but they might do for the Colorado River and the Grand Canyon. And so our motives and decisions become a little clearer.

In the concrete-scape of the south bay, nature is wrangled. Beauty and utility are considered different sides of the same coin. The beauty is extracted where it can be, and function is depleted until it can’t be. As long as the processes that we rely on keep functioning, we can try to see how much we can limit them and still keep things running. Let’s develop that landslide-prone hillside, because people might oppose higher residential buildings in the city. Let’s cover the old creek, because the land is cheap, and flooding isn’t so likely. Let’s set up a PG&E substation near town and let’s hope the hexavalent chromium kills everyone before they can sue us. That’s what it feels like.

Short-term thinking is a plague everywhere, locally and globally. Think about the parallels of NIMBYism and anti-immigration sentiments. These short-term perspectives seem to be a common line of thinking in the world of private capital, but it is seemingly becoming more common in society in general. Every man for himself, every woman for herself, and every river must be commandeered and left to dry.

We do a better job here than many large cities across the country (or so I read online), but we also tend to feel like the boons of the Earth’s geography mean we don’t have to try in the first place. This is less of a specific comment on any agency or government action, but rather just a general observation of attitudes. The local natural environment is very blessed, but that doesn’t mean we should push it to the limits by doing things like littering the area with data centers, cutting down historic tree populations for development, and continuing to use water at high rates during drought seasons. We don’t get to ignore our environment just because the hiking nearby is world class. But I guess many of the main private actors in the area actively prefer sinking money into AI companies and data centers with which people can create AI videos that use copious amounts of water. Because fuck the water, fuck the kids, I want an AI video of Tom Brady throwing a football across the Grand Canyon, while the real Colorado River stops draining. Yes, our priorities are crystal clear.
