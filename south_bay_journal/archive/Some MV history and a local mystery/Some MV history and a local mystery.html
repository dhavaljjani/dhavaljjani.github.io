Some MV history and a local mystery

It's not exactly Hounds of the Baskervilles - but we can have some fun!

Originally posted on substack on April 26, 2025

At the Mountain View public library on Tuesdays, between 1 and 5 PM, a dimension to the past can be found. The history center opens up upstairs, and a portal to bygone times is available. Old government documents, fire insurance maps, original Ohlone tools, informational pamphlets, high school yearbooks, submitted photos, previous city magazines, aerial imagery, and more from the local area overflow in the two small rooms in which they reside.

I’m lucky enough to work on Castro Street right by the library, so I’ve been able to check out the historical resources twice so far. And hopefully I’ll be going back many more times, because what I’ve picked up about local history there could not have been found or learned in any other way. I mean, did you know that the California state reptile is a desert tortoise due to the eager students of Benjamin Bubb School in Mountain View, who brought up the matter all the way up to President Nixon.

Most of the collection present in the archives can’t be taken or checked out of the library, and while lots of it is digitized on California Revealed and the Internet Archive , I recommend you go there in person for the full experience plus the expertise present in the librarians (especially the wonderful James).

I’ve spent some time looking through the collection, and I thought I could share some of my favorite historical findings so far, along with any contextual information I can find. And a reminder; this isn’t even close to half the stuff I had seen, which doesn’t even encompass like a quarter of the total collection. I highly recommend going there for yourself if you find this interesting, or have lived in Mountain View and the surrounding areas for any amount of time!

In a small book whose name has escaped me since I saw it, I found this old map of Cupertino from all the way back in 1887.

The gorgeous hand drawn compass aside, this map shows some really cool history. In the center, we can see the intersection between Stevens Creek Boulevard and a road that is shown as “Mountain View” to the north, and “Saratoga” to the south. Both make sense as names of the road, to show drivers where the roads can take them, but it was later renamed to Sunnyvale-Saratoga Road. However, eventually the City of Cupertino changed this as well. Cupertino was not a huge fan of having a major thoroughfare of their city named after two neighboring cities - hence the portions of this road that go through Cupertino were renamed De Anza Boulevard, named after Juan Bautista de Anza of the Spanish Empire. Fun fun.

We can also see the Calabazas Creek cutting through the land on the right, and what is dubbed the “Cupertino Creek” on the left. You may recognize it by the name Stevens Creek instead though.

Additionally, if you look at all the parcels and lots shown you’ll see the names of people who presumably owned the land. Their ownership of the land lends itself to so many modern streets and parks and schools that are named after these people and their families today. Here’s some I noticed; let me know if you find more!

  • Miller Middle School named after “P Miller”

  • Wilcox High School named after “W Wilcox”

  • Collins School is present right next to the “Estate of SP Collins”. Shoutout to any other Collins Cougars reading!

  • Tantau Avenue named after “Mrs C Tantau” and “F Tantau”

  • Stelling Road named after “John Stelling”

  • Regnart Creek and Regnart Road named after “A Regnart” and “M Regnart”

  • Bollinger Road named after “C Bollinger”

  • Blaney Avenue named after “CD Blaney”

  • Hollenbeck Avenue named after “BW Hollenbeck”

  • Jollyman Park named after “FM Jollyman”

  • McClellan Ranch Park named after “McClellan”

Jollyman Park lies wholly within the property of “FM Jollyman” which makes sense but it’s still just plain cool to see.

Of all the photos I was able to look through, one caught my eye AND lacked an address or location on the back of it or online. A mystery awaiting me?

I’m sure someone out there might recognize this immediately. But for me, I had no clue. And I thought it would be a fun mystery and hunt to locate exactly which building this image is in. And boy did it send me on a wild goose chase.

So first things first, what do we notice? Aside from Mr Manfredi’s dapper outfit of course.

It’s a wall-to-wall building, and I immediately assumed it would be in downtown Mountain View, somewhere on Castro Street. Possibly University Avenue or California Street in Palo Alto, but I figured Castro Street was a better bet. I opened up Google Street View and found a potential candidate for the building in the first five minutes of looking:

Looking back as far in time as Google Street View would let me, here’s the building a little further back in 2007.

Okay, I like it. The building on the left aligns particularly well, along with the tiling of the main building’s base. But a big thing that sticks out is that the entrance is on the side in our old photo, whereas this building has it in the middle. But, it’s very likely the storefront could have been changed since the early 1900s.

Eventually, through the address of the building above, I was able to find the building history of 292 Castro Street. It was constructed in 1930 according to PropertyShark , which was helpful and aligned with the alleged date of the photo. I found the assessor's cadastral map for the parcel (158-13-46), but that was of little help too.

I looked to see if I could find anything else about JV Manfredi. I was able to find a photo on the Internet Archive of what is likely a family residence on El Camino Real . I also found a photo of a young JV Manfredi in a dress , allegedly crying. Neither were particularly helpful. The latter was slightly amusing though.

I realized that if the facade for 292 Castro Street truly was changed since the early 1900s, I could find it in the online archives. I began hunting for photos of Castro Street - primarily near the intersection with W Dana Street where this building is. After looking at dozens and dozens of historic photos of Castro Street from the early 1900s, I came tantalizingly close. A photo of exactly the cross streets I wanted… but looking east rather than west. Mr Manfredi’s store could be lying just on the other side of this photo:

Heartbroken, I realized catching a glimpse of this specific building in an old photo was near impossible. My computer’s RAM cried as I opened up dozens and dozens of photos and tabs looking. After another ten minutes, I stumbled upon a peculiar photo:

Was that… the same building? The building was flipped but I thought it could solely be the image that was flipped. The glass storefront matched up too well, and a realty company and insurance company residing in the same building but in different times made sense to me. Shame this one also lacked an address apparently. I feel it is possible that this is the same building as Mr Manfredi’s office though.

Close to giving up, I decided to use the resources I had available. I decided to try and consult with James, the superman librarian. He was glad to take a look, and upon cross-reference with some newspaper clippings…he found it. He informed me that it was 268 Castro Street - just two buildings to the right of the one I had been looking so far into. Finally, the photo had an address associated with it.

Mr Manfredi, I’ve found you! Mostly thanks to James, but still. I don’t know why your building was changed so much. I don’t know how long your office stayed at 268 Castro Street. I don’t even know if you really did cry because of that dress the photo of you as a child implied. But Mr Manfredi, from one Castro Street worker to another, your town is still here and it is thriving.
